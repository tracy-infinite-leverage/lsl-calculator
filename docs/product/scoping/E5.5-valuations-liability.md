## 1. Where E5.5 sits in the platform arc

The LSL Platform (E5) is a six-sub-epic journey from "stateless calculator" to "payroll system of record for LSL". E5.5 is the **first sub-epic where a customer logs in and gets paid value out of the platform.** Everything before it (auth + masterfile + mapping + ingestion) is plumbing. E5.5 is the tap turning on.

```
E5.1 ✅  Auth + DB scaffold        (plumbing — load-bearing)
E5.2 ☐   Employee masterfile        (plumbing)
E5.3 ☐   Pay-code mapping           (plumbing)
E5.4 ☐   Pay-period ingestion       (plumbing — dam-breaker)
E5.5 ★   Valuations + Liability     ← FIRST PRODUCT VALUE
E5.6 ☐   Reconciliation             (highest-value commercial feature)
```

For the auditor-persona buyer (the CFO / outside auditor who signs off year-end LSL provisions), E5.5 is the line item that converts the platform from a tool into a subscription. It produces a **defensible liability number as at any chosen date** — the artifact the auditor needs to sign off the financial statements.

E5.6 (reconciliation) is the commercial peak, but E5.6 cannot exist without E5.5's valuation primitive — every reconciliation row is a comparison between "what was paid" and "what the engine says should have been paid", and that second term IS an E5.5 valuation.

## 2. The two customer-visible surfaces

### Surface A: On-demand valuation
A payroll manager has a single employee who's about to take long-service leave or be paid out on termination. They want a defensible number. They log in, pick the employee, pick the date, pick the reason, click. They get back:

- Weekly LSL value (the rate the employer pays per week of leave)
- Daily LSL value
- Total entitlement at the chosen date (weeks × weekly rate)
- A citation block — "this is computed under s.X of the [State] Long Service Leave Act 1955; sub-section Y applies because the employee's classification is Z"

The citation block is load-bearing for product credibility: payroll managers will not trust a number they can't defend in a meeting. The existing rules engines already emit citations; E5.5 just plumbs them to the UI.

### Surface B: Liability snapshot (the auditor's deliverable)
A CFO or auditor wants the total LSL liability across the organisation as at, say, 30 June (end of Australian financial year). They log in, pick the date, pick the scope (whole org / by team-tag / one employee), click. They get:

- A scrollable table — one row per in-scope employee with their weekly/total values
- Roll-ups by jurisdiction (NSW liability, VIC liability, ...) and by team tag
- A grand total
- A PDF download (A4, methodology footer with citation summary, page X of Y)
- A CSV download for spreadsheet work

This is the artefact that goes in the audit file. It is also the artefact that makes the platform a **recurring** revenue surface — every reporting cycle (monthly / quarterly / annually) the customer runs this again. Once they've used the platform for one year-end, switching costs are high.

## 3. Operator Round-1 decisions (locked 2026-05-29)

Three questions resolved before this re-author. Each affects what ships in v1.

### OQ-VAL-1 — Cross-state employees: how do we value them?

**The problem.** Some employees move between states during their continuous service — e.g. an employee starts in Sydney (NSW), transfers to Melbourne (VIC) after three years, retires at twelve years. The LSL Acts do not have a single, uniform "portable LSL across states" mechanism (portable LSL exists within a few specific industries, not as a default). So when we value this employee's LSL, which state's rules engine governs?

**Options considered:**
- (a) **Auto-split** — run NSW for years 1-3, VIC for years 4-12, concatenate. Sounds clean, but the statute doesn't bless any concatenation rule, and the result becomes brittle if a state changes its accrual formula retroactively.
- (b) **Manual nomination** — operator picks one jurisdiction. The engine runs that jurisdiction's rules against the entire service period.

**Operator decision: (b) — manual nomination.** Default selection in the UI is the jurisdiction at `valuation_date - 1 day` (i.e. "what state were they in just before the valuation date?"). Operator can override.

**Why this matters commercially.** Putting the decision in the operator's hands gives them a single defensible engine call to point to in audit — "we valued this employee under VIC because that's where they were employed at termination, and here's the statute that gives us that answer." Auto-split would force the platform to take a position the statute itself doesn't take, and that position would be a perpetual liability vector.

### OQ-LIA-1 — Liability reports: do we filter by tags?

**The problem.** A CFO wants "LSL liability for the finance team as at 30 June". The platform needs a mechanism to slice employees into groups. We could (a) force everyone into a single classification column, (b) use `default_work_jurisdiction` as a de-facto grouping, or (c) add a free-form `tags` column that lets the customer organise employees however they want (team, cost centre, project, contract group, etc.).

**Operator decision: (c) — tags ship as v1.** A `tags` text[] column lands on the `employees` table in E5.2 (the masterfile sub-spec is scope-amended). E5.5 liability reports support filtering by one or more tags.

**Implication for E5.2.** The masterfile sub-spec gains a v1 column (`tags`) that it didn't have at scoping time. **The amendment is pending — it sits in `stash@{0}` on the PM machine as of 2026-05-31 and will be committed alongside the E5.5 spec when the operator authorises a documentation commit, or folded into the E5.2 rebase work the operator approved as decision 5.**

### OQ-LIA-2 — Terminated employees in liability reports: included or hidden?

**The problem.** Most "as at 30 June" liability reports are run after some employees have already left the organisation. Their `end_date` is before the as-at date. What should the report do — hide them, show them with their last-known accrued value, or show them with $0?

**Options considered:**
- (a) **Hide** — only show currently-active employees. Risk: auditor can't see the rows that were considered and excluded.
- (b) **Show with last-known value** — show as if they were still employed at as-at. Risk: misrepresents statute (their accrual stopped at `end_date`).
- (c) **Show with $0** — present in the report, value $0, with implicit "considered and computed". Aligns with statute.

**Operator decision: (c) — strict $0.** The terminated row appears in the liability report with `total_value = $0.00`. No exclusion. No warning. The auditor sees the row and the explicit zero.

**Why this matters.** It's the statutorily-correct answer. The auditor's job is to verify the platform considered every employee in scope and computed the right number. (c) gives them that. (a) hides rows, (b) lies about the number.

**The catch — Round-2 follow-up (OQ-LIA-2a, still open).** There's a small but real UX risk: a user looking at a $0 row might think "this is a bug" instead of "this is by statute". So an open question remains about whether to add a banner ("N terminated employees in scope, all $0 by statute") and/or a filter chip ("hide terminated rows" — defaults ON, opt-in to see). See §4 below.

## 4. Still-open question: OQ-LIA-2a (NOT BLOCKING)

How do we make the silent-$0 statute rule visible to the user without overriding it?

| Option | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| (a) Always-on banner ("[N] terminated employees in scope at $0 per statute s.X") | Visible, undismissable, can't miss it. | Adds chrome to every report, including reports with zero terminated rows in scope. |
| (b) Filter chip "Hide terminated rows", defaults ON | Cleaner default view. Auditor opts in to see all. | Hides rows by default. Mild risk an auditor doesn't realise the rows exist (especially if there's no count badge on the chip). |
| (c) Both — banner shows the count, chip controls visibility | Safest. Acknowledges rows exist regardless of visibility state. | More UI surface than strictly needed. |

**PM recommendation: (c).** Modest UI cost; both signals (count visible + control visible) match the auditor-persona's mental model. The cost of getting this wrong (auditor signs off on an incorrect liability because they didn't realise terminated rows were $0 by statute, not by mistake) is high; the cost of (c) is small.

**Status: NOT BLOCKING the spec → plan handoff.** Plan/tasks can proceed assuming (c) is the default; swapping to (a) or (b) later is a one-component change.
